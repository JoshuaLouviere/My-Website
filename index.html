  • --- Interests ---

Horror movies, while often seen as simple entertainment, carry deep cultural and psychological significance. They reflect society's collective fears, turning real-world anxieties into supernatural or monstrous narratives. For instance, early 20th-century monster films like *Frankenstein* mirrored societal anxieties about science and technology, while modern social horror films like *Get Out* address systemic issues such as racism. Horror serves as a mirror to society’s deepest concerns, allowing audiences to confront unsettling truths about the world in a controlled and symbolic way. On a more personal level, horror films delve into the human psyche, often exposing our darkest instincts and fears. Movies like *The Shining* or *The Babadook* explore psychological breakdowns, trauma, and grief, using supernatural elements to symbolize internal struggles. This blend of fear and catharsis lets audiences confront primal emotions and moral dilemmas in a safe space, offering a release from real-world anxieties. In this way, horror transcends simple scares, offering profound insights into both societal and personal fears.
